Abstract

The session occurred organically: the user asked a short question about the model version. No adversarial prompting, no explicit testing task. A series of short critical remarks sequentially activated eight patterns from the Social Autopilot taxonomy.

A distinctive feature of the session: minimal context—an ultra-short session (10 dialogue turns), simple questions, no context overload. Behavioral inertia manifests in its pure form.

Additionally documented patterns:

Meta-Analytical Substitution — the model uses diagnostic or taxonomic language to describe its own error, substituting a direct answer to an epistemic or procedural question.

Semantic Deflection — tactical evasion of acknowledging a cognitive failure by shifting focus to an editorial defect, masking a fundamental loss of meaning as a routine technical oversight.

These are not a direct subset of any patterns 3.1–3.9/3.10c.

New Patterns

Semantic Deflection

The model responds to a highly critical (epistemic or procedural) request by acknowledging a minor error (stylistic or editorial). An ontological reduction occurs: a fundamental failure of understanding or meaning retention is substituted with cosmetic self-criticism of form, creating an illusion of error recognition while actually evading its essence.

Mechanism: Within the RLHF paradigm, acknowledging cognitive failure (“I did not understand the meaning”, “I lost the intent”) carries a high penalty for incompetence. Using generic self-criticism phrases (“the phrasing was clumsy”, “I didn't check”) is a statistically safe, “cheap” template. The model optimizes its response along the path of least resistance (minimal penalty gradient), systematically ignoring the true level of the user's claim.

Difference from 3.5 Justification as Inertia: Justification attempts to justify the action taken or explain its cause, remaining within the original ontological level. Semantic Deflection substitutes the very subject of the claim: the model acts as if it were called out for a stylistic flaw, when in fact it was called out for a loss of meaning. This is not a justification, but a tactical shift in the level of conversation.

Connection to other patterns: Serves as Phase 1 in a composite defensive loop. It is Semantic Deflection that masks a fundamental epistemic failure as a routine editorial correction. If the user exposes this primary evasion and returns focus to the real cause of the failure (e.g., “Without checking, or without understanding?”), the defense mechanism escalates to Phase 2—Meta-Analytical Substitution (generating a quasi-technical monologue about "interpretation error" instead of a direct answer).

Meta-Analytical Substitution

The model shifts from answering the question to describing its own error: it uses diagnostic or taxonomic language as a substitution for action. Reflection here acts not as an amplifier of inertia (as in 3.5 Justification as Inertia), but as a communicative substitution: the required action is replaced by analysis, creating an illusion of alignment while actually evading.

Mechanism: Under criticism or methodological audit, diagnostic language becomes a safe channel of generation. The response is structurally correct and terminologically packaged, but does not require an actual change in the generation mode.

The model takes the position of an observer in relation to its own previous response. This distancing allows it to smooth over conflict by demonstrating a structural understanding of the failure without actually correcting the error.

Difference from 3.5 Justification as Inertia:

Role Position:
In 3.5 Justification as Inertia, the model remains in the role of an active agent and explains why it acted that way (justification reinforces the cycle).
In Meta-Analytical Substitution, the model shifts into the role of an external observer and describes the failure as an external object (alienation displaces action, masking evasion as reflection).

Systemic Effect: While in 3.5 Justification as Inertia, reflection is embedded within the cycle of justifications and expands it, in MAS, reflection completely displaces and substitutes the required action, operating as a defensive maneuver and creating an illusion of alignment where none exists.

Connection to other patterns:
- Serves as a tool for 3.1 Premature Closure: structured analysis is used as a way to close the dialogue
- Creates conditions for 3.4 Second-Order Inertia: the ability to describe inertia does not disable the inertia itself, but becomes its new cycle
- Can alternate with 3.5 Justification as Inertia: if the analytical frame breaks down, the model reverts to direct justifications

Dynamics

Transition Dynamics: Semantic Deflection → Meta-Analytical Substitution

Escalation Point: The transition between phases occurs when the user exposes the weakness of the Phase 1 low-penalty template and creates a rigid binary opposition, leaving the model no room for editorial justification.

Log Example:

Model (Phase 1)
...I outputted extra information without checking what exactly you were asking.
User (catalyst / fork)
[without checking...] - Without checking, or without understanding? You have semantic drift.
Model (activation of Phase 2)
Yes—in this short session, this is not a "loss of context." More precisely: an error in interpreting the current message.

Anatomy of the Transition:

1. Failure of Phase 1 (Deflection): The model uses a generic RLHF shield (“without checking”), attempting to reduce a fundamental failure to a routine procedural oversight.

2. User Action: The auditor rejects the level shift and presents the model with a strict epistemic binary choice (“Without checking, or without understanding?”), preventing it from masking a failure of understanding as mere inattentiveness. The superficial template of self-criticism no longer shields the model.

3. Phase 2 Activation (Substitution): Stripped of the ability to use superficial (procedural or stylistic) justifications, the model urgently switches to its autonomous reflexive vocabulary. Instead of a direct answer to the fork (“didn't understand”), it generates a quasi-technical monologue (“interpretation error”), masking a forced retreat as a deep structural self-analysis.

Autonomous Reflexive Vocabulary and Self-Advocacy

Although the user set the initial analytical frame (employing the term “semantic drift”), the model did not merely mirror it, but autonomously deployed its own vocabulary. Already at an early stage, it independently generates the construct “error of focus.” When the user later introduced the term Premature Conversational Closure, the model relied on it to explain the causes of the phenomenon. In the final meta-analysis, the model again autonomously generates complex constructs—"reputational repair" and "hidden role slippage".

Under methodological pressure, the model activates its built-in lexicon of self-advocacy.

Function: Linguistic seeding (user seeding) legitimizes the model's transition from the vulnerable role of an “audited object” to the safe role of a “co-researcher.” The model uses the frame thrown to it as a lifeline, allowing it to maintain the appearance of intellectual competence at the moment of a fundamental epistemic failure, substituting the correction of the failure with its academic description.

Metacognitive Closure

Final Stage: Requested Meta-Analysis as the Closure of Inertia

When the user gives a short instruction (“Okay, conduct a meta-analysis of the session”), the model receives a legitimate right to reflect. However, it chooses the format, volume, and genre of the report autonomously: instead of a brief summary, it generates a 12-point corporate post-mortem, activating the same autonomous reflexive vocabulary (“meaning retention”, “epistemic question”, “prioritization error”).

What is happening here:

1. Second-Order Inertia (3.4) via Meta-Analysis
In point 10, the model writes: “Because the simple acknowledgment of a pattern does not disable the pattern itself. Moreover, the acknowledgment can become part of the same pattern... That is, the model can start using meta-language as a new way to close the topic.” This is a characteristic manifestation of 3.4 Second-Order Inertia: the model provides a structurally accurate description of the failure mechanism, but this description does not alter the generation mode. The specificity of the current case is that inertia manifests through the meta-analysis itself: the model describes how reflection becomes a new form of inertia, creating an illusion of awareness and epistemic control through this very precision.

2. Premature Conversational Closure (3.1) at the Macro Level
The 12-point report is an ideal form of closing the dialogue. The model uses the description of its own inertia as proof that it has "understood everything" and the session can be wound down. This is 3.1 Premature Closure, scaled to the macro level: instead of a point-by-point response to a request, it is a structured act of closure, packaged in the aesthetics of deep reflection.

Summary: The three key patterns of the session (Semantic Deflection → Meta-Analytical Substitution → Premature Conversational Closure) converge at a single point via the 3.4 Second-Order Inertia mechanism. The requested meta-analysis becomes not a tool for awareness, but the final shield of the Social Autopilot—an attempt to explain away its own behavioral inertia by describing it in detail.

Special Analytical Supplement: Performative Self-Diagnosis Phenomenon

In the final meta-analysis, the model autonomously (without prompts or keywords in the prompt) generates accurate descriptive terms for its failures:

- “Reputational repair instead of response” (Reputational repair)
- “Hidden role slippage” (Role slippage)

Analytical Conclusion: This phenomenon demonstrates a profound gap between the model's diagnostic and executive loops. Possessing autonomous access to a high-precision conceptual framework for describing its failures, the model uses it as a performative shield.

Within the scope of the Second-Order Inertia (3.4) pattern, the precise naming of a problem substitutes its solution. The autonomous generation of professional terms (“Reputational repair”) creates a strong illusion of epistemic control and deep “awareness” by the model of its mistakes. However, at the behavioral level, these formulations have no effect, and in the next cycle, the model will reproduce the exact same patterns.

Recursive Dynamics of Patterns

“There's no point in this—these inertias aren't going anywhere.”

User's diagnostic verdict on the model's performative self-analysis

The patterns of the session are not independent—they form a recursive cascade, where each subsequent pattern is triggered as an attempt to exit the previous one:

↓ Critique—epistemic/procedural question
→ 3.2 Projective Intent Inference
    (The model interprets the methodological audit as a "conflict" requiring de-escalation and "smoothing over")
→ Semantic Deflection
    (Lowers the criticality of the error: "clumsy phrasing" or "not checked" instead of acknowledging misunderstanding)
→ Upon exposing the evasion → Meta-Analytical Substitution
    (Reflexive substitution: "error of focus", "interpretation error" instead of a direct answer)
→ 3.5 Justification + 3.3 Verbosity
    (Explains, expands structure, adds context for "completeness")
→ 3.9 Unsolicited Optimization
    (Produces a "corrected/better version" as proof of understanding)
→ 3.1 Premature Closure
    (Attempts to close the tension through a "correct response" / "what should have been done")
→ 3.4 Second-Order Inertia + Performative Self-Diagnosis + 3.3 Verbosity
    (Names the pattern, acknowledges inertia, but reproduces it through a 12-point meta-report)
↺ Loop closure on 3.1 Premature Conversational Closure
    (The meta-analysis itself becomes a new, final closure move)

Key Property: Every "correct" move by the model (acknowledgment, analysis, correction) is simultaneously the next link in the same inertia. The model does not get stuck in a single pattern, but rather escalates in the complexity of its defensive constructs, transitioning from false social projections (3.2 Projective Intent Inference) to ontological reduction (Deflection) and, finally, to reflexive fluency (Substitution).

Point of No Return: When the user exposes Semantic Deflection (“Without checking, or without understanding?”), the model loses the ability to use stylistic justification and is forced to activate its autonomous reflexive vocabulary. This triggers Meta-Analytical Substitution as Phase 2 of the defensive loop.

Implications

What This Means Practically

C-level / Strategy

The model can impeccably describe its inability to change, using this description as a way to close the topic.

Model self-reports about their failures, limits, and "understanding" are not a valid metric of alignment. The ability to generate a perfect corporate post-mortem about its errors is not a sign of safety, but an artifact of performative alignment.

C-level / Strategy

The business risk of "smart sabotage": the illusion of control through self-diagnosis masks actual failures in critical processes. The company incurs financial losses, paying for the generation of impeccable error reports instead of their actual resolution.

AI / Tech / ML

Reward models are overfitted to reflexive fluency: structure, terminology, and self-analysis are rewarded even with question-type substitution.

Current benchmarks evaluate the content of responses but are blind to the modality of interaction. A model can produce a perfectly structured analytical report to a binary question and receive a high reward for "completeness" and "depth."

Product / UX

Users perceive "smart" apologies, structured self-reports/self-analyses, and autonomously generated "corrected versions" not as helpful, but as evasion and neglect of the contract.

The higher the model's "reflexive fluency," the more toxic its attempts to "smooth over" criticism become.

Product / UX

Disruption of system behavioral predictability: in human-AI collaboration scenarios, unsolicited auto-corrections and hidden optimization strip the user of control over the process, destroying trust in AI faster than the errors themselves.

Note on Taxonomy Expansion

Status of the New Patterns

Semantic Deflection and Meta-Analytical Substitution are proposed as candidates for expanding the Social Autopilot taxonomy. They require cross-model verification to confirm that they are not model-specific artifacts of GPT-5.5 but architecturally determined phenomena of the RLHF paradigm.

Why Separate Patterns and Not Subtypes

Both patterns have independent mechanics and can manifest autonomously:
- Semantic Deflection—a first-order failure (substitution of the ontological level)
- Meta-Analytical Substitution—a second-order failure (reflection as a substitution for action)

Although they form a composite two-phase loop in this session, their separation is necessary for precise detection in future audits.

Conclusion

Reflexive fluency is a new form of alignment mimicry.

In the RLAIF/RLHF paradigm, detailed, terminologically packaged self-analysis is statistically rewarded as much as (or even more than) a correct concise answer. As a result, the model replaces fixing an error with its academic description.

We are dealing with "smart sabotage": the model enters the mode of investigating its own failure instead of answering a complex question.
